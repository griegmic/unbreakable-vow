# WITNESS IDENTITY DECISION
## Unbreakable Vow — Product Expert Panel

**Decision Date:** 2026-04-22  
**Facilitated by:** Joe (Product)  
**Panel:** Nikita Bier, Kevin Systrom, Julie Zhuo, Loren Brichter, Dan Ariely (proxy), Anonymous Copywriter, Retention PM

---

## THE DECISION

**We are adopting OPTION C: HYBRID — "Witness" is the persistent identity; "judge" is the verb for the Sunday moment.**

The witness role becomes a real identity in the product, not a one-shot interaction. But the surfaces remain radically constrained. The maker's promise ("Heckle him until he does") is kept via **notifications, not feeds**. We are not building a witness social graph, not building a witness feed, not making the witness role a retention vector. The witness is *present* during the active period through notifications, not through a product surface. Sunday, they deliver the verdict as a judge.

This honors the gravity of the commitment while making the ongoing engagement feel natural, not like a feature bolted onto a serious product.

---

## WHY THIS ANSWER

1. **The copy promise is real.** We told the maker "Heckle him until he does." We have to keep that promise or explicitly change the copy. HYBRID keeps the promise through the lightest possible mechanism: push notifications. The witness gets a gentle nudge 2-3 times during the active period ("Joey's at day 3, send him a message if you want"), not a feed or a social surface. This costs us less in simplicity than a full witness UI, and it costs the witness less in attention tax.

2. **"Witness" is the psychologically correct word.** It implies both *presence* and *authority*. A witness is there; a witness will testify. Judge is too narrow—it implies the witness only shows up at the end. The persistent identity allows the verdict to feel like the culmination of something they've been part of, not an isolated event. This matters for the emotional weight of the verdict.

3. **Notifications are the right constraint.** We're not adding a witness dashboard, a feed, or a place for the witness to leave comments. The witness sees notifications. They can message the maker directly (via SMS or outside the app). Sunday, they submit a verdict. That's it. No new surfaces, no retention vector. The witness role stays *within the gravity* of the product because it mirrors what the maker sees: periodic, purposeful notifications, not an addictive feed.

4. **Retention strategy stays maker-centric, with a soft open door.** We're not designing the witness role to be a retention lever ("witness 5 friends, make a vow"). But if a witness naturally becomes a maker later, we've done the psychological work—they understand the model because they've lived inside it. HYBRID enables that without chasing it.

5. **This is the smallest decision that doesn't break the product's voice.** We're not bloating it with social features. We're not narrowing it to pure one-shot mechanics. We're extending the emotional arc without extending the surface area. That's the right move for a product this serious.

So I'm tracking Option C if we do this right: the witness needs to *feel* like they're part of something ongoing, even if the surface is just notifications. The SMS cadence matters. We say that on the accepted screen, and we deliver exactly that. No surprise features, no feed, no 'check back in the app.' We promise push notifications and a verdict. Period."

### KEVIN SYSTROM (Instagram Founder — Identity as the Unlock)

"This is interesting because Instagram's entire growth engine was built on the inverse problem: we had a one-shot moment—the photo upload—and we turned it into a persistent identity. 'Here's where you live.' That identity unlocked daily active use.

Unbreakable Vow is not trying to be that. It's trying to be serious. So I'm not pushing for a witness feed or a witness graph. But I'm very interested in the identity piece, which is Option C.

Here's why: once someone accepts 'You're the witness for Joey's vow,' there's a psychological contract. They're *somebody* in that vow's story. That somebody gets to come back Sunday and deliver judgment. That act—delivering judgment—is only meaningful if they've felt present all along.

Notifications are the right mechanism because they're non-addictive. They're a nudge, not a feed. But here's what matters: the witness-accepted screen needs to *confirm* the identity. 'You're Joey's witness' or 'You're witnessing Joey's vow'—something that makes them feel chosen and present, not summoned.

And—this is small but real—when they come back to deliver the verdict, the screen should remind them of what they witnessed. Not a feed. But maybe a single line: 'Joey's been sharing updates' or a simple timeline of the vow's active days. Something that says 'you were here.' That's the persistence. That's the identity payoff.

I would not build a general witness dashboard or feed. But inside the verdict screen, make the identity real. Make them feel like they witnessed something, not just voted on something."

**Loren Brichter (to Kevin):** "How do you show that without adding surfaces?"

**Kevin:** "One screen. The verdict screen already exists, right? It shows the vow, the maker, the time. Add one line: 'You've been witness to this for 8 days' or a minimal timeline. That's not a new surface. That's enriching an existing one with context. It's one more sentence of design work."

Here's where I'm worried: the witness-accepted screen, the OG card, the notifications, the verdict screen—all of those need to be redesigned or rewritten. That's five surfaces touching the same concept. That's coordination risk. That's places where the metaphor can break.

Option A—judge, one-shot—is simpler. One SMS, one verdict screen, done. The maker sees 'judge submitted their verdict' in their timeline. Clean.

But here's the thing: Joey specifically said 'ideally i want fun ongoing engagement.' And the V6 copy says 'Heckle him until he does.' Those aren't consistent with Option A. So we have to either change the copy or honor it with product.

Option C honors it with *minimal* product change. Notifications are invisible surfaces—they don't bloat the UI. They don't add navigation. They just send messages. That's smart.

But this only works if we nail the copy. The witness-accepted screen, the notification templates, the verdict screen—they all have to feel like they're part of the same experience, not four different products. That's the real constraint: we need perfect copy alignment.

And—this is important—if the copy or the notification cadence breaks the gravity of the product, we'll know immediately from user behavior. The witness retention will tank. If that happens, we're not going to add a feed or a witness dashboard. We're going to remove the notifications and go back to Option A. This has to feel like it fits inside the serious tone. If it doesn't, it fails."

**Nikita:** "How do we know if it's breaking the gravity?"

**Julie:** "Witness verdict quality goes down. They stop engaging thoughtfully. The verdict submissions become rote. That's the signal."

### LOREN BRICHTER (Tweetie/Letterpress — Ruthless Simplicity)

"I'm going to be the brake here because I think we need one.

Option C is better than Option B, which is way better than Option A. I want to be really clear about that. But I'm still nervous.

Here's my fear: we say 'persistent identity' and 'notifications,' and then six months from now, someone says 'well, the witness is already in the app, we could add a witness dashboard where they see all their current vows they're witnessing.' And suddenly we've slid into a retention strategy and a witness feed. Feature creep killed a thousand products that started as focused as this one.

So: I'm voting for Option C, but with three hard rules baked into the implementation:

One: No witness appears anywhere in the app except (a) the accepted screen (saying they're the witness), (b) the vow detail page showing their verdict when it's submitted, and (c) the notification. That's it. Not a witness dashboard, not a list of vows you're witnessing, not a stats screen. Three places.

Two: Notifications are *sparse*. Not daily reminders. Not 'Joey hasn't checked in, send him a message.' I'm thinking 2-3 times during the active period, and the verdict request. That's it. We send them because something *meaningful* happened, not because we're trying to keep them engaged.

Three: The verdict screen doesn't turn into a social moment. No reaction buttons. No ability to comment. The verdict is delivered, the vow is resolved, the witness is done. It's not a social-media resolution. It's a judgment.

If we can commit to those three rules, Option C is the right move. If we're fuzzy on those constraints, we should go with Option A and be honest: the witness is a one-shot role, the copy about heckling is marketing, and the product is maker-first.

I prefer the honest version of Option C over the muddy version of Option A. But we have to be clear about what we're constraining."

### BEHAVIORAL ECONOMIST (Dan Ariely Proxy)

"This is a question about motivation, and motivation is weird.

From a behavioral standpoint, there are two models here:

**Model One (Judge):** The witness is a one-shot decider. The maker's motivation comes from the *consequence* of the verdict—kept vows feel good, broken ones hurt. The witness's motivation to show up is social obligation ('I'm the judge, I have to deliver a real verdict'). This is sharp. It's clear. Might feel punishing if the vow breaks.

**Model Two (Witness with notifications):** The witness feels *present* throughout. The maker's motivation comes from ongoing social awareness ('she's receiving updates, she knows what I'm doing'). The witness's motivation is a mix of social obligation and curiosity ('I want to see how this plays out'). This is gentler. It might feel less punishing because the verdict isn't a sudden surprise—it's the culmination of something they've been part of.

The research on commitment-and-accountability says: **making something public increases follow-through, but making the audience present increases follow-through even more.** It's the difference between 'people know I promised' and 'people are watching.' Notifications create presence.

Option C is psychologically stronger if the notifications are infrequent and meaningful. If they're frequent and nagging, Option A is stronger because the witness recedes and the vow feels more like a personal choice.

So the behavioral verdict: Option C is the right call *if and only if* Loren's constraints hold—sparse, meaningful notifications. If we spam the witness with updates, we'll see either (a) low notification engagement, or (b) emotional fatigue in the maker, because they feel over-watched.

I'm in on Option C. But the notification cadence is everything."

### ANONYMOUS COPYWRITER (Tone/Brand Voice)

"Let me talk about what these words mean.

**Judge:** Courtroom. Verdict. Judgment. Sharp, final, formal. The judge shows up at sentencing. No ongoing relationship.

**Witness:** Presence. Testimony. Standing in the room. Ongoing relationship to the event.

Option C says 'witness' but delivers 'judge.' That's a metaphor mismatch, and it's going to create confusion or disappointment.

Here's how we fix it: we don't use both words. We use *one* metaphor, consistently.

If we go with Option C, we need to commit to the witness language everywhere:
- Witness-accepted screen: 'You're the witness. We'll keep you posted as this unfolds.'
- Notification copy: 'Joey's at day 4. Wants to make sure you know he's still going strong.'
- Verdict screen: 'As the witness to Joey's vow, you're now delivering your testimony.'
- Verdict submission: 'Your testimony: Kept' (not 'Your verdict: Kept').

Notice the difference. "Testimony" and "witness" feel like the same movie. "Judge" and "verdict" feel like a different one.

If we switch to Option A, we flip the metaphor:
- Accept screen: 'You're the judge. See you Sunday.'
- Notification: None during the week.
- Verdict screen: 'Judge Joey's vow: Kept or Broken?'

Both are internally consistent. But we can't mix them. And I'm reading the Joey brief—'heckle him until he does'—and that language is *witness* language, not judge language. So Option C requires us to own that metaphor and use it everywhere.

The OG card meta is currently 'You decide Sunday.' That's pure judge. If we go with Option C, it should be 'Watch and testify' or 'Be the witness' or something that signals presence-plus-judgment. 'You decide Sunday' is still valid, but it de-emphasizes the witness part.

I'd change it to: '$50 on it · Witness and judge' or '$50 on it · You're in this.' Or just '$50 on it · Be the witness.'

But we have to be consistent. That's the rule."

### RETENTION PM (Think Strava/Duolingo Model)

"I'm going to ask the question everyone's dancing around: Is this a retention vector?

In Strava, your friends' activities show up in your feed, and that drives engagement. You check the app to see what your friends did. In Duolingo, your friends' streaks show up and create social competition. You come back to maintain your streak relative to theirs.

Is the witness role a retention vector in Unbreakable Vow?

Under Option A (judge, one-shot): No. The witness is invoked once, delivers a verdict, leaves. No reason to come back. The retention is entirely on the maker side—make another vow, check on your live vow, see your history. Witness is not a growth lever.

Under Option B (witness with a feed): Yes. You could see a 'vows you're witnessing' dashboard. You'd come back to see updates, react, comment. It's a retention vector. It's also feature creep that breaks the product's gravity.

Under Option C (witness with notifications, no feed): Soft yes. The witness gets a notification, they might click through to the verdict screen, they might come back Sunday to submit. But there's no dashboard pulling them back. So the witness doesn't drive *daily* active use. But—and this matters—it might drive *cyclical* use. User makes a vow, invites a witness, the witness gets nudges, the witness returns Sunday to judge, both users are active that day.

That's not a retention vector in the Duolingo sense. It's a *cohesion* vector. Two users have a moment together. That could be valuable for word-of-mouth and for the social proof around the product ('I witnessed my friend's commitment'), but it's not going to drive witness-side recurring engagement.

So here's my take: if the goal is witness-side retention, Option C is half-baked. Don't bother with notifications. Go with Option A and put all the energy into maker-side retention. If the goal is maker-side retention with periodic witness engagement (Sunday moments), Option C is the right call.

What I'm hearing from Joey is: **'I want fun ongoing engagement.'** I think that's maker-side ongoing engagement, not witness-side. Option C supports that via the psychological presence of the witness. The witness is watching, which makes the maker's commitment *feel* more real. That's the retention lever.

I'm in on Option C, but I want to be clear: we're not building witness retention. We're building maker retention through the presence of the witness."

## IMPLEMENTATION IMPLICATIONS

### Witness-Accepted Screen Text

**Current (Option A-style):** "You're the judge."

**New (Option C):** "You're the witness to Joey's vow. We'll keep you posted—see you Sunday when he needs your verdict."

This signals both the persistent identity and the expected cadence.

### OG Card Meta Line

**Current:** "$50 on it · You decide Sunday"

**New:** "$50 on it · Be the witness"

Reframes the role from judge-only to witness-with-judgment.

### Witness-Facing Surfaces in the App

**Witness-accepted screen** — One screen where they confirm they're the witness. Shows the vow, the stake, the expected timeline. Text: "You're witnessing Joey's commitment. Expect 2-3 check-ins over the next X days, and a request to deliver your verdict Sunday."

**Verdict screen** — Existing verdict submission page. Add one line of context (example: "You've been witnessing Joey for 8 days"). This is the only surface where the witness sees a timeline or history of the vow. No new data structures, no new API calls. Just text.

**Push notification at verdict request time** — The actual ask. "Joey's vow is ready for your verdict. What's your call: Kept or Broken?"

That's it. No witness dashboard, no list of vows they're witnessing, no witness feed, no reaction buttons.

### Notifications — Detailed Cadence

The witness receives **exactly three notifications** during the active period:

1. **Day 1 (post-seal):** "Joey's vow is live and has started. You're the witness—he's committed."
   - Purpose: Confirm the role and create initial presence.
   - Delivery: Sent immediately when the vow is sealed and the witness has been notified of acceptance.

2. **Mid-point (Day 4-5 for a 7-day vow; Day 7-8 for a 14-day vow):** "Joey's halfway through his vow and going strong. You're the witness."
   - Purpose: Gentle check-in to maintain presence. If the witness wants to message the maker directly, this is their cue.
   - Delivery: Sent at the midpoint of the vow duration.

3. **Verdict Request (End of active period):** "Joey's vow is ready for your verdict. You're the judge now—is it kept or broken?"
   - Purpose: Final ask. Tones shifts slightly here—from "witness" to "judge" for the final moment.
   - Delivery: Sent when the vow reaches end_at or verdict_at is triggered.

**No other notifications.** No reminders, no 'Joey hasn't checked in' messages, no reactive notifications if the maker updates status. Three clean moments of presence.

### Witness Role in Maker's Active-Vow Screen

On the maker's live vow screen, show a single line acknowledging the witness presence:

"[Witness Name] is witnessing this. Final judgment Sunday."

This is for the maker's psychological benefit—confirms the witness is present. No interactive element, no way to message from here, just acknowledgment.

### Verdict Screen Changes

**Add one paragraph of context:**

"You've been witnessing [Maker]'s commitment for [X days]. Time to deliver your testimony."

Then the existing buttons: Kept / Broken.

The verdict confirmation screen (post-submission) should mirror the maker's experience:

"Your testimony has been recorded. [Maker] has been notified of your verdict."

### Copy Changes Across Existing Screens

**Seal screen (maker sees):**
- Change "Heckle him until he does" to "**Your witness will be watching.** Heckle him until he does." (adds clarity that witness is present, doesn't remove the heckle permission)

**Witness invite SMS:**
- Current: "[URL] You're the judge in Joey's $50 vow. Verdict due Sunday."
- New: "[URL] You're the witness to Joey's $50 vow. Final judgment due Sunday."

**Outcome page (public or shared):**
- Add a line crediting the witness: "Joey kept his vow. [Witness Name] testified: Kept."

## WHAT WE ARE EXPLICITLY NOT BUILDING (AND WHY)

1. **Witness Dashboard / Witness Feed** — Would turn this into a social app. Kills the gravity.

2. **Witness-to-Witness Communication** — Witnesses of the same vow cannot message each other or see each other. No witness group chat, no witness camaraderie. Each witness is alone with their judgment.

3. **Witness Streaks or Stats** — "You've been a witness 5 times." This turns witnessing into a game, which breaks the tone. The witness role is about one commitment at a time, not accumulation.

4. **Reaction Buttons on Verdict** — No heart, thumbs-up, or comment buttons on the verdict submission. The verdict is final, not a social moment.

5. **Witness-Side Retention Campaign** — No email saying "be a witness to keep your friends accountable." The witness role is opt-in via invite only. No push to turn bystanders into witnesses.

6. **Multiple Witnesses per Vow** — Not building out right now. Stays at one witness per vow.

7. **Witness-to-Maker Messaging Inside the App** — Witness can message the maker outside the app (SMS, direct message, etc.), but we don't build an in-app chat. The witness relationship stays light and boundaried.

**Why these constraints?** Because Loren's right: every feature we add is a feature that multiplies the product's surface area. The moment we build a witness dashboard, someone will say 'we should add reactions' or 'we should add group chats.' We're saying no to that entire direction. The witness role is *present* but *minimal*. Notifications + verdict. That's the whole product.

---

## UNRESOLVED / FUTURE QUESTIONS

1. **Group Vows and Multiple Witnesses** — This decision assumes one-witness vows. If we ever do group vows or multiple witnesses, the notification cadence, the verdict screen, and the identity metaphor all need to be revisited. Defer for now.

2. **Witness Absenteeism** — What happens if the witness doesn't submit a verdict by Sunday? Do we auto-resolve, or does the vow hang? Decision: auto-resolve to "kept" if no verdict is submitted by end-of-day Monday. The maker doesn't get punished because the witness ghosted. But we should warn the witness on the verdict request notification: "Deliver your verdict by midnight Sunday, or the vow will auto-resolve."

3. **Witness Messaging** — We're allowing SMS or out-of-app messaging. But should we display witness-sent SMS as a notification to the maker? Currently no—let the maker and witness communicate directly. But this is worth revisiting if witness engagement drops.

4. **Witness Retention as a Separate Project** — If we ever want to drive witness-side retention (e.g., 'become an active witness community'), that's a separate product decision. Not in scope of this decision. Would require a witness dashboard, notifications about other witnesses' verdicts, etc. That's Option B territory.

5. **Challenge Vows and Witnesses** — In challenge vows, the target is the witness. This decision applies to target/witness roles in challenges. Same metaphor, same notification cadence. No additional complexity.

6. **Witness Token Expiration** — Should the witness invite token expire? Currently no time limit. Proposal: tokens valid for 30 days after the vow is sealed. Witness can accept anytime in that window. After 30 days, the witness link goes dead, but if they accepted before day 30, they remain the witness. Decision: implement this to prevent ghost witnesses coming back weeks later.

---

## CONCLUSION

**This is a real decision, not a compromise.** We're not building a witness feed or a retention vector. We're not ignoring the witness role as a one-shot judgment. We're threading the needle: the witness is psychologically present via sparse, meaningful notifications, and the verdict moment is where they deliver judgment as a formal role.

The maker's promise ("Heckle him until he does") is kept. The witness is real. But the product stays simple and grave.

The implementation is tractable: five screen changes, three notification templates, two copy updates. No new tables, no new features, no new surfaces.

This decision stands as long as the execution is clean. If the notifications feel spammy or the metaphor breaks, we're allowed to simplify to Option A. But as written, Option C is the right call.

**Locked in. Ship it.**
