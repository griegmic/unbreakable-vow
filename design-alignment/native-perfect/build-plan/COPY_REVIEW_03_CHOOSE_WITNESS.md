# Copy Review: Screen 03 — Choose Witness
**Date:** 2026-04-30  
**Reviewer:** Copywriter  
**Source of Truth:** `project-perfect-final-build-mocks.html` shot 5, STEP_9 screen-03 spec, WEB_TO_NATIVE_COPY_MATRIX, FINAL_RAVE_WORTHY_AUDIT, COPY_PARITY rules.

---

## 1. H1 Headline

**What it says now:** "Choose your **witness.**" (serif 600/48, "witness" in italic gold `em`)

**What I'd change it to:** Keep as is. It's right.

**Why:** The headline is the legal contract. "Choose" is imperative and direct—it doesn't hedge or soften. "Your witness" makes this personal and consequential. The italic gold em on "witness." (with period) is the brand voice made visible: serif is ceremony, the period is finality. No hesitation. Compare to alternatives like "Pick your judge" (weaker—"pick" is less formal), "Get a witness" (passive-aggressive), or "Who will hold you accountable?" (it's therapy speak, not this brand). The current version is solemn, direct, and inevitable. Do not change.

---

## 2. Sub-Headline Below H1

**What it says now:** "Stay true or pay the price. They decide."

**What I'd change it to:** Alternative 1: "Stay true or pay the price. They decide." (current — keep)  
Alternative 2: "Keep your word or pay the price. They'll call it."

**Why:** The current line is already sharp and high-conviction. "Stay true" is slightly more emotional than "keep your word," which plays well into the solemn tone. "They decide" is the product promise — not "they judge," not "they'll verify," but "they decide." Simple power transfer. Alternative 2 emphasizes the finality with "They'll call it" (sharper action verb), but it adds a syllable and loses the elegance of "decide" as a standalone.  
**Recommendation:** Current version is stronger. Keep it.

---

## 3. The "READY TO SEAL" Decision Card Kicker

**What it says now:** "READY TO SEAL"

**What I'd change it to:** Keep as is.

**Why:** At 3/5 progress, the user is not "almost done"—they're at the inflection point. "READY TO SEAL" signals that the next step after witness is authentication and payment, then the actual seal. It's aspirational without being false. Alternatives: "YOUR VOW" (is generic and loses the seal/commitment beat), "WHAT YOU'RE STAKING ON" (too descriptive, kills the energy), "THE VOW" (flat). "READY TO SEAL" is both a label and a subliminal message—you're ready. The user feels the forward momentum. Correct.

---

## 4. MetaGrid Labels

**What it says now:** "STAKE" / "IF BROKEN" / "VERDICT"

### 4a. "VERDICT"

**What it says now:** "VERDICT" (value: "Sunday")

**What I'd change it to:** Keep "VERDICT".

**Why:** The label is about the user's responsibility as the definer of outcomes, not about a date. The value next to it ("Sunday") is just the date constraint. "DEADLINE" is weaker (business language), "BY" is ambiguous, "WHEN" is too informal. "VERDICT" owns the concept—this is when the judgment lands. It's solemn and exact. Correct.

### 4b. "IF BROKEN"

**What it says now:** "IF BROKEN" (value: "$50 to ALS Association")

**What I'd change it to:** Keep "IF BROKEN".

**Why:** The label must be a conditional—it's a stake, not a penalty. "PENALTY" is punitive and sounds legal, losing the personal bet tone. "GOES TO" is flat and transactional. "CONSEQUENCE" is weaker and less direct. "IF BROKEN" is the conditional promise: you flake, this happens. It's the product's core beat. Correct.

---

## 5. The "Add a witness" Judge Card Primary Action

**What it says now:**  
Title: "Add a witness"  
Sub: "Pick someone who won't let you slide."

**What I'd change it to:**  
**Finalist 1 (current):** Title: "Add a witness" / Sub: "Pick someone who won't let you slide."  
**Finalist 2:** Title: "Add a witness" / Sub: "Pick someone who won't let you off."  
**Finalist 3:** Title: "Pick your witness" / Sub: "Someone who won't let you slide."

**Why:** The current title "Add a witness" is task-focused and matches web parity. "Pick your witness" is slightly more conversational but adds friction with "your" (the user knows it's theirs). Current title is better.  
For the sub, "won't let you slide" has the right tone—slightly tough, lived-in, not motivational. "Won't let you off" is sharper but sounds more forgiving (off the hook, like they're giving you a pass). "Slide" implies you're slipping without effort. That's the right emotional match for a first-time user about to stake money on their word. The current line is right.  
**Recommendation:** Current version (Finalist 1) is strongest. Do not change.

## 6. The "Share link" Quiet Button

**What it says now:** "Share link"

**What I'd change it to:** Keep as is.

**Why:** Quiet buttons should be functional labels, not salesey. "Share link" is precise—the user understands they can send a witness invite URL to anyone, no app required. Alternatives like "Send a witness link" (adds 2 words, same meaning), "Text someone the link" (assumes SMS; share sheet is more versatile), "Invite by link" (too formal) all say the same thing with more friction. "Share link" is terse, clear, and consistent with iOS language. Correct.

## 7. The "Decide later" Quiet Button

**What it says now:** "Decide later"

**What I'd change it to:** Keep as is.

**Why:** The button defers the witness selection to a checkpoint later in the flow (D10, before payment). "Decide later" is perfectly terse and clear—no explanation needed. Alternatives like "Skip for now" (sounds optional, lowers commitment), "I'll pick after I seal" (too wordy, assumes user knows the seal comes next), "Witness later" (missing the action verb) all lose ground. The current label is right.

## 8. The Footer Trust Line

**What it says now:** "Nothing charges unless you break it."

**What I'd change it to:**  
**Finalist 1 (current):** "Nothing charges unless you break it."  
**Finalist 2:** "No charge unless you break it."  
**Finalist 3:** "Nothing charges if you keep your word."

**Why:** This line appears on multiple screens (stake, payment, seal). The spec says it's a trust statement. "Nothing charges unless you break it" is calm and assumes best case (you'll keep it). It's already sharp.  
Finalist 2 ("No charge") is more direct but loses the calm tone—it sounds like a warning.  
Finalist 3 ("if you keep your word") flips the framing to positive, which is emotionally warmer but slightly less powerful at this moment. The user is about to enter auth/payment, so negative framing (unless you break it) is more grounding.  
**Recommendation:** Current version (Finalist 1) is strongest. Keep it.

## 9. The Progress Indicator

**What it says now:** "3 / 5" (at top center)

**What I'd change it to:** Keep "3 / 5".

**Why:** Numeric progress is the fastest read. The user sees 3/5 and knows they're 60% through. The bar confirms it visually. Evocative alternatives like "Almost there" are false (60% is not "almost"), and no number creates ambiguity. At this stage, the user has invested vow + stake + 10 minutes. They want to know where they stand, not be patronized with progress language. The numeric read is clearest and most honest. Correct.

**What it actually SHOULD say per STEP_9 spec:** "Share link" + **"Decide later"** (NO "Go solo" visible on this screen).

The "Go solo" path exists only via D10/D11 (the witnessless checkpoint). This screen should not offer it. The copy for "Decide later" is correct (Finalist 1 in #7 above).

## Three Highest-Impact Recommendations

### 1. **Lock the button visibility issue first (Go solo → Decide later)**
**Impact:** High—shipping "Go solo" on screen 03 creates a UX fork that breaks the spec. The button must be hidden and only reachable via D10 checkpoint. This affects user flow and the witness-decision logic. Fix the mock deviation before any native build starts.

### 2. **Confirm "won't let you slide" is final**
**Impact:** Medium—the judge card sub-line is the emotional centerpiece of this screen. It's already strong ("won't let you slide" is lived-in, slightly tough-love), but if there's any hesitation about tone (is it too harsh for first-time users?), test it with one alternative: "Pick someone who believes in you." However, my read is that "won't let you slide" matches the brand (solemn, direct, slightly dangerous). Keep it.

### 3. **Verify "READY TO SEAL" isn't premature**
**Impact:** Low-Medium—at 3/5 progress, the user hasn't yet authed or paid. "READY TO SEAL" feels aspirational but accurate (the next step after witness *is* seal). However, if user research shows this creates false confidence before the payment/auth step, consider "READY TO CONTINUE" or just "YOUR VOW." Current version is stronger—keep it.

## Unifying Thesis

**The soul of this screen:** The witness is not a feature—they are the user's final consequence. This screen must shift the user from "making a vow to myself" to "making a vow in front of someone." The copy does this through one decision: who will you trust to judge you? Every word is designed to make that decision feel weighty and inevitable, not optional or friendly. "Choose your witness. Stay true or pay the price. They decide."

**Is the current copy serving that soul?** Yes, completely. The headline is imperative (choose, not suggest). The sub is high-stakes (stay true or pay). The judge card is direct ("won't let you slide" — not "will support you"). The trust line is matter-of-fact (nothing charges unless you break). There is no hedging, no softening, no motivational language. The product is commitment under accountability. The copy owns it. Do not soften or rewrite for taste. This is right.

**One gap to fix before graduation:** The mock still shows "Go solo" on this screen, but the spec removes it (P-9). This is a parity issue, not a copy issue, but it affects the perceived journey. Once "Go solo" is hidden and the "Decide later" button takes its place, the screen is correct.
